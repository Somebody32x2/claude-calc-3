Triple integrals & round coordinates

Nothing new happens here — that is the good news. A triple integral is the same slicing move you already know, run one dimension higher: chop the solid, measure each slice, add them up. The only genuinely new thing is what a tiny box looks like in round coordinates, where the volume element picks up factors of r and ρ² sin φ for reasons you can see.

Slice, measure, add

Cut the solid with parallel planes. Each cut is a flat region whose area you can already find. Sweep the cut through the solid and accumulate — a double integral inside a single one.

dV = r dz dr dθ
Cylindrical: polar plus height

For anything built around an axis. It's the polar tile of Lab 06 with a height glued on, so the same lone r comes along unchanged.

dV = ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ
Spherical: two curved edges

A spherical box has one straight edge and two arcs, and one of those arcs shrinks near the poles. That is where ρ² and sin φ come from.

01

Slicing a solid

Exactly the sweeping-blade move from Lab 02, one dimension up. There the blade exposed a curve and we integrated its area; here it exposes a flat region and we integrate its area.

Take a solid and cut it with a horizontal plane at height z. The cut is a two-dimensional region, and its area A(z) is something a double integral can find. Stack up all those slices, each of thickness dz, and you get the volume:

V = ∫ A(z) dz   where   A(z) = ∬slice at z 1 dA

Every solid below is a shape of revolution, so its slice is a disk of some radius R(z) and A(z) = πR(z)² — no work required. Sweep the blade and watch the running volume fill toward the exact answer. The general ∭ f dV is the same thing with a density f inside instead of a bare 1.

02

Cylindrical: polar with a height

If a solid is built around an axis — a can, a cone, a paraboloid bowl — describe its base in polar and keep z as it is. The volume element is the polar tile you already earned, extruded.

Cylindrical coordinates are just (r, θ, z): polar in the xy-plane, with z left completely alone. Because the z direction is untouched, the area factor from Lab 06 carries over unchanged and simply gains a thickness:

dA = r dr dθ  ⟹  dV = r dr dθ · dz = r dz dr dθ

The cell below is a curved brick: two flat radial faces, two curved ones, and flat top and bottom. Its exact volume is the polar wedge area times the height, ½[(r+dr)² − r²] dθ dz, and as the steps shrink that lands on r dr dθ dz. Shrink the sliders and watch the gap close — it is the same ½dr/r story as before, because it is literally the same geometry.

03

Spherical: where ρ² sin φ comes from

This is the factor everyone drops. It is not arbitrary — it is the product of three edge lengths, and one of those edges shrinks to nothing at the poles. Move φ and watch it happen.

Spherical coordinates locate a point by distance from the origin ρ, the angle down from the north pole φ (the polar angle, from 0 to π), and the angle around the axis θ (the azimuth, 0 to 2π):

x = ρ sin φ cos θ   y = ρ sin φ sin θ   z = ρ cos φ

Now build a tiny box by stepping each coordinate a little. Its three edges are not all the same kind of thing:

• Stepping ρ moves you straight outward — that edge has length dρ.
• Stepping φ swings you along a line of longitude, a circle of radius ρ — that arc has length ρ dφ.
• Stepping θ swings you around a line of latitude. That circle does not have radius ρ — it has radius ρ sin φ, the distance from the axis, not from the origin. So that arc has length ρ sin φ dθ.

dV = (dρ)(ρ dφ)(ρ sin φ dθ) = ρ² sin φ dρ dφ dθ

The interactive makes the third bullet concrete. The violet circle is the line of latitude through your point; its radius is printed live. Slide φ toward either pole and that radius collapses toward zero — which is exactly why sin φ, and not something constant, multiplies the volume. At the equator (φ = 90°) the factor is at its largest, sin φ = 1.

05

Common misconceptions

Every one of these is a mistake that looks completely reasonable at the time. Reading them once, before you need them, is worth more than any number of solved examples.

✕ the myth
"Spherical coordinates use ρ² — so a bigger sphere just scales the answer by ρ²."
✓ actually

The ρ² is not a scale factor applied at the end; it lives inside the integral and is integrated over. That is why the ball's volume comes out as R³/3 rather than anything proportional to R². Whenever a factor depends on a variable of integration, it must go inside.

✕ the myth
"φ and θ are interchangeable — they're both just angles."
✓ actually

They play completely different roles, and only one of them appears in the volume element. φ measures down from the axis and controls how big the latitude circle is; θ runs around that circle. Swap them and you get sin θ, which is wrong and — worse — negative for θ > π. Physics and maths texts also swap the two names, so always check which convention a source uses before trusting a formula.

✕ the myth
"A triple integral gives volume."
✓ actually

Only when the integrand is 1. ∭ 1 dV is volume; ∭ δ dV with a density is mass; ∭ f dV in general is an accumulation of whatever f measures. The same confusion appears one dimension down, where a double integral is a volume only when the integrand is a height.

✕ the myth
"The limits can go in any order as long as I get them all."
✓ actually

The innermost integral may have limits depending on the outer variables; the outermost must have constant limits. If an outer limit still contains a variable, the setup is wrong and the answer will not be a number. Reordering is legitimate — but it means re-describing the solid, not shuffling the symbols.

✕ the myth
"r in cylindrical and ρ in spherical are the same thing."
✓ actually

They differ by exactly the factor this lesson is about. r is distance from the z-axis; ρ is distance from the origin. They are related by r = ρ sin φ — the same ρ sin φ that shows up as the latitude-circle radius. Mixing them is the single most common algebra slip when converting between the two systems.

✕ the myth
"Use spherical coordinates whenever there's a sphere in the problem."
✓ actually

Choose coordinates to make the boundaries constant, not to match a keyword. A cylinder cut by a sphere is often easier in cylindrical, and a region bounded by planes stays easiest in Cartesian even if a sphere is mentioned. The test to apply is: in which system do my limits become numbers?
