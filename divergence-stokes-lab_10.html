Divergence & Stokes

The finale, and the punchline is that there is only one theorem here. Green's theorem, the divergence theorem and Stokes' theorem all say the same sentence: what leaks out through the boundary equals what is generated inside. They differ only in the dimension of the thing you are integrating over — and once you see the shared skeleton, the formula sheet collapses from three entries to one.

∯S F · n dS
Flux through a surface

How much field flows out through a closed surface per unit time. Same idea as Lab 09's flux, with a 2-D skin instead of a 1-D curve.

∭E div F dV
Sources inside

Divergence measures creation at a point. Add it up over the solid and — the divergence theorem — it equals the flux out of the shell around it.

∮C F · dr = ∬S curl F · n dS
Stokes: Green, tilted

Circulation round a loop in space equals the curl passing through any surface that the loop bounds. Green's theorem, freed from the plane.

01

The divergence theorem, live

A solid, and the field streaming through its skin. The flux out of the surface and the divergence summed over the interior are computed separately — and land on the same number.

The statement, sometimes called Gauss's theorem:

∯S F · n dS = ∭E (∇ · F) dV   where  ∇ · F = Px + Qy + Rz

Think of F as the flow of a fluid. The left side counts what crosses the boundary going outward — net escape. The right side adds up, over every interior point, how much fluid is being created there. If nothing is created inside, nothing can escape; if the interior is manufacturing fluid, it has to come out somewhere. The theorem is conservation of stuff, written in calculus.

For a sphere of radius a, the outward normal is beautifully simple: it points radially, so n = ⟨x, y, z⟩ / a. That one fact turns most sphere flux integrals into something you can do in your head — which is exactly what the worked example below exploits.

02

Stokes: the surface doesn't matter

The strangest and most useful part. Fix a loop in space; every surface that shares that loop as its rim gives the same curl integral. Morph the surface and watch the number refuse to move.

Stokes' theorem says circulation around a closed curve C equals the flux of the curl through any surface S whose boundary is C:

∮C F · dr = ∬S (∇ × F) · n dS

The left-hand side never mentions S — it only knows about the rim. So the right-hand side cannot depend on which surface you pick. Below, the rim is the unit circle in the xy-plane, and the slider morphs the surface from a flat disk up into a tall dome. The curl flux through it is recomputed from scratch at every step, and it does not budge — because the boundary integral it must equal never changed.

03

One theorem, three costumes

Line them up and the pattern is unmistakable. In every case: integrate a derivative over a region, or integrate the original thing over that region's boundary. Same answer.

Every one of these — including the fundamental theorem of calculus you met in first-year — is an instance of a single statement: ∫region (derivative) = ∫boundary (original). The derivative changes name as the dimension goes up (ordinary derivative, then curl, then divergence), and the boundary drops a dimension each time, but the shape of the sentence never changes.

1-D region, 0-D boundary

Fundamental theorem

∫ab f′(x) dx = f(b) − f(a)

Integrate the derivative along an interval; get the original function at the two endpoints. The "boundary" of a segment is just its two ends.

2-D region, 1-D boundary

Green's / Stokes'

∬S (∇×F)·n dS = ∮C F · dr

Integrate the curl over a surface; get the circulation round its rim. Green's theorem is this with the surface lying flat in the plane.

3-D region, 2-D boundary

Divergence theorem

∭E (∇·F) dV = ∯S F · n dS

Integrate the divergence over a solid; get the flux out through its skin. The same cancellation argument as Lab 09, one dimension up.

The proof is the same in all three cases too, and you already saw it: chop the region into cells and note that everything interior cancels. Each internal face is shared by two cells, counted once with each orientation, so it contributes nothing. Only the outer skin survives. That single argument, run in one, two and three dimensions, produces this whole family — and in the language of differential forms it really is written as one line, ∫Ω dω = ∫∂Ω ω.

04

Worked by hand, with the numbers

Flux of F = ⟨x, y, z⟩ out of the sphere of radius a

Done twice — directly off the surface, then by the divergence theorem. The second way takes one line.

  1. The direct way. On the sphere the outward unit normal is radial:
    n = ⟨x, y, z⟩ / a
    So the integrand is
    F · n = ⟨x,y,z⟩ · ⟨x,y,z⟩ / a = (x² + y² + z²)/a
    But on the sphere x² + y² + z² = a² exactly, so
    F · n = a²/a = a   (a constant!)
  2. Integrate over the surface. A constant integrand just multiplies by the surface area, and a sphere's area is 4πa².
    ∯ F · n dS = a · 4πa² = 4πa³
    = 4πa³
  3. Now the divergence theorem. Compute the divergence:
    ∇ · F = ∂(x)/∂x + ∂(y)/∂y + ∂(z)/∂z
            = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3
  4. Integrate that over the ball. Again constant, so it is 3 × volume, and the ball's volume is 4πa³/3:
    ∭ 3 dV = 3 · (4/3)πa³ = 4πa³
    = 4πa³ ✓ same answer
  5. Check it live. Set the field to ⟨x,y,z⟩ and the solid to a sphere above. At a = 1 both readouts show 12.566 = 4π.

Use Stokes to find ∮C F · dr for F = ⟨−y, x, 0⟩ around the unit circle

The rim sits in the xy-plane. We'll pick the easiest surface it bounds — the flat disk.

  1. Compute the curl. Use the determinant form with P = −y, Q = x, R = 0:
    ∇ × F = ⟨ Ry − Qz,  Pz − Rx,  Qx − Py ⟩
    Working through each slot:
    Ry − Qz = 0 − 0 = 0
    Pz − Rx = 0 − 0 = 0
    Qx − Py = ∂(x)/∂x − ∂(−y)/∂y = 1 − (−1) = 2
    ∇ × F = ⟨0, 0, 2⟩
  2. Choose the surface and its normal. Take the flat unit disk in the plane z = 0. For a counterclockwise rim, the right-hand rule gives an upward normal:
    n = ⟨0, 0, 1⟩
  3. Dot them.
    (∇ × F) · n = ⟨0,0,2⟩ · ⟨0,0,1⟩ = 2
  4. Integrate over the disk. Constant again — 2 × area, and the unit disk has area π:
    ∬ 2 dS = 2 · π(1)² = 2π
    = 2π ≈ 6.283
  5. The point of Stokes. Any other surface with the same rim — a hemisphere, a cone, a wobbling dome — gives the identical 2π. Drag the dome slider in section 02 and watch it hold at 6.283 while the surface changes shape completely.
05

Common misconceptions

✕ the myth
"The divergence theorem works for any surface."
✓ actually

The surface must be closed — it has to fully enclose a solid, with no openings — and oriented outward, and the field must be smooth everywhere inside. A hemisphere alone is not closed; you must add the flat disk that caps it. And the smoothness clause bites exactly as it did in Lab 09: the inverse-square field F = ⟨x,y,z⟩/ρ³ has divergence zero everywhere it is defined, yet its flux out of any sphere around the origin is 4π, not 0 — because of the singularity at the centre. That single example is the whole of Gauss's law in electrostatics.

✕ the myth
"Curl and divergence are both just 'the derivative' of a vector field."
✓ actually

They extract completely different information and even return different types. Divergence ∇·F is a scalar — how much is created here. Curl ∇×F is a vector — how fast and about which axis things spin here. A field can have plenty of one and none of the other: ⟨x,y,z⟩ is pure divergence with zero curl, while ⟨−y,x,0⟩ is pure curl with zero divergence.

✕ the myth
"In Stokes' theorem you have to use the surface you were given."
✓ actually

You may replace it with any surface sharing the same boundary curve, and that freedom is the entire practical value of the theorem. Faced with curl flux through an ugly paraboloid, swap in the flat disk with the same rim. The one thing you must keep consistent is the orientation: the normal has to follow the right-hand rule relative to the direction you traverse the rim.

✕ the myth
"Zero flux out of a surface means the field is zero inside."
✓ actually

It means the net creation inside is zero — sources and sinks could be cancelling, or the field could simply be flowing straight through, in one side and out the other. The rotation field ⟨−y,x,0⟩ is vigorous everywhere and yet has exactly zero flux out of any closed surface, because it never leaves: it just circulates. Zero flux constrains the total, not the field.

✕ the myth
"Green's, Stokes' and the divergence theorem are three separate formulas to memorize."
✓ actually

They are one statement in three dimensions of dress: the integral of a derivative over a region equals the integral of the original over that region's boundary. Even the fundamental theorem of calculus is the 1-D member of the family. Memorizing three formulas invites mixing them up; remembering one sentence and asking "what is my region, what is its boundary, which derivative belongs here?" does not.
