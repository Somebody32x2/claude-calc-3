Hills, valleys & saddles

To find the highest and lowest points of a landscape, you look for where the ground goes flat — where every partial derivative is zero. But flat is a trap: the top of a hill, the bottom of a valley, and the middle of a saddle are all flat. The whole art is telling them apart, and there is one clean number that does it.

∇f = 0
Find the flat spots

A high or low point can't be tilted — if it were, you could step uphill or downhill and do better. So every interior extremum has fx = fy = 0. Solve that to get your candidates.

flat ≠ extreme
But flat isn't enough

A saddle is flat too, and it is neither a max nor a min. Zero gradient only makes a point a suspect. You still need to look at how the surface curves away.

D = fxxfyy − fxy²
One number decides

The discriminant reads the curvature in every direction at once. Its sign — together with the sign of fxx — sorts every flat spot into peak, pit, or pass.

01

Where the ground goes flat

The dots mark every critical point — every place ∇f = 0. On the map below they're exactly where the little gradient arrows shrink to nothing. Pick one; its horizontal tangent plane shows just how flat it is.

Why must an extremum sit at ∇f = 0? Freeze y and you have an ordinary one-variable function of x; at its peak, its derivative fx is zero — that's Calc 1. The same holds freezing x, forcing fy = 0. So at any interior high or low point, both partials vanish and the tangent plane lies perfectly horizontal. The catch, which the next section fixes, is that plenty of non-extreme points satisfy this too.

02

The test that tells them apart

Zoom in on a critical point and the surface looks like a bowl, an upside-down bowl, or a saddle — decided entirely by its second derivatives. The discriminant packages that decision into arithmetic.

Near a flat point the linear part is gone (that was the whole point of ∇f = 0), so the surface's shape is set by the quadratic term of its Taylor expansion: ½(fxxh² + 2fxyhk + fyyk²) for a small step (h,k). That expression is a quadratic form, and whether it opens up, opens down, or does both at once is governed by the matrix of second partials — the Hessian.

H = [ fxx  fxy ; fxy  fyy ]    D = det H = fxxfyy − fxy²

Read it like this. If D > 0 the surface curves the same way in every direction — a genuine bowl — and the sign of fxx says which way up: positive is a minimum, negative a maximum. If D < 0 it curves up one way and down another — a saddle. And if D = 0 the test is blind: the curvature is too flat in some direction to call, and you need more than second derivatives. The equivalent, deeper statement is about the Hessian's two eigenvalues — the curvatures along its special perpendicular axes — whose signs are what D and fxx are really reporting.

03

Optimizing on a leash: Lagrange

Often you can't roam the whole plane — you're pinned to a curve, like "spend exactly this budget." The extrema then land where a level curve of f is tangent to the constraint, and that tangency has a tidy algebraic form.

Walk along the white constraint curve g = 0 and watch the coloured contours of f slide past. While you're cutting across contours your value is still changing, so you're not done. You stop changing only at the instant the constraint runs along a contour — tangent to it. There, the two gradients line up: ∇f = λ∇g. That is the whole method of Lagrange — the mysterious multiplier λ is just the ratio of the two aligned arrows.

The mistake everyone makes once. Reading D = 0 as "it's a saddle" or "it's nothing." It means neither — it means this test has no opinion. The surface x⁴ + y⁴ has a clear minimum at the origin yet D = 0 there; the monkey saddle has D = 0 too and is genuinely a saddle. Same number, opposite truths. When D = 0 you have to go back to the function itself — higher derivatives, or just checking values along a few directions — because second-order information has run out.

05

Common misconceptions

Optimization is where confident-looking work goes wrong most often, usually by finding the right critical points and then answering the wrong question with them.

✕ the myth
"To find the maximum of f on a region, solve ∇f = 0 and compare the answers."
✓ actually

That misses the boundary entirely. The argument for ∇f = 0 only works at an interior point, where you're free to step in any direction. On the edge of a region you are not free — the maximum can sit on the boundary with the gradient still pointing firmly uphill, straight out of the region. Think of a tilted plane over a square: it has no critical points at all, yet it certainly has a highest point, at a corner. The complete recipe on a closed bounded region is: interior critical points, plus the boundary analysed separately (often with Lagrange), plus the corners — then compare every candidate's value. Skipping the second and third steps is the most common way to lose the actual answer.

✕ the myth
"D = 0 means it's a saddle" — or "means it's nothing."
✓ actually

It means the test has no opinion. Both readings are wrong, and provably so: x⁴ + y⁴ has an unmistakable minimum at the origin with D = 0, while the monkey saddle also has D = 0 and is genuinely a saddle. Same number, opposite truths. When D = 0, second derivatives have run out of information and you must go back to the function — higher-order terms, or just evaluating along a few directions.

✕ the myth
"A local maximum is the maximum."
✓ actually

"Local" means it beats its immediate neighbours and nothing more. The double well above has two separate minima; a hill-climbing method that stops at the first flat spot it finds will happily report a local answer as though it were global. Finding the global extremum means enumerating all the candidates and comparing their actual values — the second-derivative test classifies points but never ranks them.

✕ the myth
"λ in Lagrange is just bookkeeping — solve for it and throw it away."
✓ actually

λ has a real and useful meaning: it is the rate at which the optimal value changes as you loosen the constraint. If the constraint is a budget, λ is what one more unit of budget is worth — economists call it the shadow price. In this lab's example the two multipliers come out as exactly 1 and 2, matching the constrained values f = 1 and f = 2. Far from disposable, λ is often the most interesting number in the problem.

✕ the myth
"Lagrange tells you which point is the max and which is the min."
✓ actually

It produces candidates — every point where the constraint curve runs tangent to a level curve — and stays silent about which is which. You still have to plug each one back into f and compare. The method finds where the value stops changing; deciding whether that stall is a peak or a trough is a separate, and much easier, final step.

✕ the myth
"Every critical point is an extremum of some kind."
✓ actually

Saddles are critical points and are neither. In fact on the four-corners surface above, half the critical points are saddles. "Critical" is a statement about the first derivatives being zero — it identifies where the ground is flat, which includes mountain passes just as much as summits and basins.
