Double integrals

Last lab asked how the landscape tilts at a point. This one asks the opposite question: how much is there in total? Tile the ground with tiny rectangles, stand a column on each one as tall as the surface above it, and add up the volumes. Shrink the tiles to nothing and the sum stops being an estimate — it becomes ∬R f(x,y) dA.

Σ f(xᵢ, yⱼ) ΔA
Add up columns

Chop the region into a grid. Each cell gets a box of base ΔA and height f sampled somewhere inside. The sum of the boxes approximates the volume.

∬R f dA
Take the limit

Let the cells shrink. If the sum settles on one number no matter how you sample, that number is the double integral — a signed volume, not an approximation.

∫∫ f dx dy
Do it one axis at a time

Nobody evaluates a limit of sums by hand. Fubini's theorem says you can integrate in x, then integrate the result in y — two Calc 1 problems, nested.

04

Computing them by hand

Work inside out. The inner integral treats the outer variable as a constant — exactly the habit you built with partial derivatives, run backwards. Its limits may involve the outer variable; the outer integral's limits must always be plain numbers, and the answer must be a number too.

∫₀² ∫₀¹ (x + y) dx dy
inner dxy is a constant here
→ [x²/2 + xy]₀¹ = ½ + y
outer dynow an ordinary integral in y
→ [y/2 + y²/2]₀² = 1 + 2 = 3
∫₀¹ ∫₀ˣ 2y dy dx
inner dylimit depends on x — that's fine
→ [y²]₀ˣ = x²
outer dxthe x's must all be gone by now
→ [x³/3]₀¹ = 1/3
∫₀¹ ∫ᵧ¹ e^{x²} dx dy
inner dxe^{x²} has no elementary antiderivative — stuck
swapsame triangle, other order: 0≤x≤1, 0≤y≤x
→ ∫₀¹ ∫₀ˣ e^{x²} dy dx
→ ∫₀¹ x e^{x²} dx = (e−1)/2

The mistake everyone makes once. Sketch the region before you write a single limit. Reversing the order of integration is not swapping the two d's and leaving the numbers where they are — it is re-describing the same region with strips turned ninety degrees, which almost always produces different limits. The third example above only becomes solvable because the swap was done properly.

05

Common misconceptions

Four ideas that feel like common sense and are not. The first one accounts for more wrong signs than anything else in the subject.

✕ the myth
"A double integral gives the volume under the surface."
✓ actually

It gives signed volume. Anywhere f dips below the xy-plane, the columns count as negative and subtract. The x·y example above integrates to exactly zero over a symmetric square — not because the solid is empty, but because equal amounts sit above and below. If you genuinely want geometric volume regardless of sign, integrate |f| instead.

✕ the myth
"To reverse the order, swap the two d's and leave the limits alone."
✓ actually

Reversing the order means re-describing the region with strips turned ninety degrees, and that almost always produces different limits. Swapping the symbols while keeping the numbers silently changes which region you are integrating over — and gives a confidently wrong answer. Always sketch the region first; the limits are read off the picture, never off the previous set-up.

✕ the myth
"Limits of integration always have to be constants."
✓ actually

Exactly backwards for the inner one. The inner limits may — and for any non-rectangular region must — depend on the outer variable. It is the outer limits that must be plain numbers. A useful self-check: after the inner integral, every trace of the inner variable should be gone, and after the outer one, you should be holding a number.

✕ the myth
"Fubini's theorem says the order never matters."
✓ actually

It says so when the function is well behaved — continuous on the region, or more generally absolutely integrable. That covers everything you will meet in this course, so in practice you may swap freely. But the hypothesis is not decoration: there are genuine functions whose two iterated integrals give different answers, and they are the reason the theorem has conditions at all.
