Green's theorem

Here is a genuinely surprising claim: what happens on the edge of a region is completely determined by what happens inside it. Walk the boundary once, adding up how much the field pushes you along — and you get exactly the total amount of spin contained in the interior. One integral around a curve equals another integral over the whole area it encloses.

∮C F · dr
Circulation round the edge

Walk the boundary counterclockwise, accumulating F · dr as in Lab 08. This measures how much the field carries you around the loop.

∬R (Qx − Py) dA
Total spin inside

The 2-D curl at a point says how fast a tiny paddlewheel there would spin. Add that up over the whole region — and it matches the boundary walk exactly.

interior edges cancel
Why it's true

Chop the region into tiny cells. Every internal edge is walked twice, in opposite directions, so it cancels. Only the outer boundary survives.

01

The two forms, side by side

Pick a field and a region, then switch between circulation and flux. In both cases the boundary number and the interior number are computed independently — and they agree.

Green's theorem comes in two flavours that look different and are really the same statement rotated by ninety degrees. The circulation form measures the field's push along the boundary and equates it to the curl inside. The flux form measures the field's flow through the boundary and equates it to the divergence inside:

circulation:  ∮C P dx + Q dy = ∬R ( ∂Q/∂x − ∂P/∂y ) dA
flux:         ∮C F · n ds = ∬R ( ∂P/∂x + ∂Q/∂y ) dA

Read the two interior quantities as physical things. Curl Qx − Py is local spinning — drop a paddlewheel and see if it turns. Divergence Px + Qy is local source-or-sink — is fluid being created here or swallowed? The theorem says: the total spin inside shows up as circulation on the rim, and the total source inside shows up as net outflow through the rim. Nothing can hide in the interior.

02

Why the interior cancels

The proof idea in one picture. Chop the region into cells and walk every cell's boundary counterclockwise. Shared edges get walked twice in opposite directions — they annihilate.

Suppose Green's theorem holds for a tiny cell. Now tile the whole region with such cells and add up all their little circulations. Any edge interior to the region is shared by two neighbouring cells, and because both are traversed counterclockwise, that shared edge is walked once in each direction. Those two contributions are equal and opposite, so they cancel exactly. What survives is only the edges with no neighbour — the outer boundary. Meanwhile the right-hand sides simply add up to the integral over the whole region. Slide the grid finer and watch the interior arrows pair off.

03

Worked by hand, with the numbers

Both directions, fully written out: once using Green's theorem to avoid a nasty boundary integral, once checking it the long way.

Evaluate ∮C (−y) dx + x dy around the unit circle

The classic. Done twice — directly, then via Green's theorem — to see the theorem earn its keep.

  1. The direct way: parametrize the circle. Counterclockwise, r(t) = ⟨cos t, sin t⟩ for t from 0 to 2π.
    x = cos t,  dx = −sin t dt
    y = sin t,  dy = cos t dt
  2. Substitute into the integral. With P = −y = −sin t and Q = x = cos t:
    P dx + Q dy = (−sin t)(−sin t dt) + (cos t)(cos t dt)
                  = sin²t dt + cos²t dt
                  = 1 dt
  3. Integrate.
    ∫₀^{2π} 1 dt = 2π
    = 2π ≈ 6.283
  4. Now Green's theorem instead. Compute the curl once:
    ∂Q/∂x = ∂(x)/∂x = 1
    ∂P/∂y = ∂(−y)/∂y = −1
    curl = 1 − (−1) = 2
  5. Integrate the curl over the disk. The curl is the constant 2, so the double integral is just 2 × area — no integration needed at all.
    ∬R 2 dA = 2 · area(disk) = 2 · π(1)² = 2π
    = 2π ✓ same answer
  6. The moral. The direct route needed a parametrization, a trig substitution and the Pythagorean identity. Green's route needed two partial derivatives and the area of a circle. When the curl is simple, always go inward.

Flux of F = ⟨x², xy⟩ out of the unit square [0,1] × [0,1]

A non-constant divergence, so the interior integral is a genuine double integral — but still far easier than four separate edge integrals.

  1. Compute the divergence. With P = x² and Q = xy:
    ∂P/∂x = ∂(x²)/∂x = 2x
    ∂Q/∂y = ∂(xy)/∂y = x   (x is constant here)
    div F = 2x + x = 3x
  2. Set up the double integral over the square.
    ∬R 3x dA = ∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ 3x dy dx
  3. Inner integral (over y). There is no y in the integrand, so it just multiplies by the length of the interval:
    ∫₀¹ 3x dy = 3x · [y]₀¹ = 3x · 1 = 3x
  4. Outer integral (over x).
    ∫₀¹ 3x dx = [ 3x²/2 ]₀¹ = 3/2 − 0
    = 3/2 = 1.5
  5. What we avoided. The direct flux computation means four separate line integrals — bottom, right, top, left — each with its own outward normal ⟨0,−1⟩, ⟨1,0⟩, ⟨0,1⟩, ⟨−1,0⟩. Doable, but four chances to slip a sign. The interior route was one short double integral. Set the region to the square above and read 1.500 off the panel.
04

Common misconceptions

✕ the myth
"Green's theorem works for any closed curve."
✓ actually

Three hypotheses genuinely matter. The curve must be closed and simple (not self-intersecting); it must be positively oriented — counterclockwise, so the region stays on your left; and P, Q must have continuous partials everywhere inside, not just on the curve. That last one is the killer: the vortex field ⟨−y, x⟩/(x²+y²) has zero curl but blows up at the origin, so applying Green's theorem to a loop around the origin gives the wrong answer of 0 instead of the true 2π. If the field misbehaves anywhere inside, the theorem does not apply — no matter how nice the boundary looks.

✕ the myth
"Curl measures whether the field goes in circles."
✓ actually

It measures whether a tiny paddlewheel placed at that point would spin — which is not the same thing. The shear field ⟨y, 0⟩ flows in perfectly straight horizontal lines and has curl −1 everywhere, because the flow is faster at the top of the paddlewheel than the bottom. Conversely a field can curve around the origin and still have zero curl. Curl is about local differential rotation, not the global shape of the streamlines.

✕ the myth
"Orientation is a technicality you can fix at the end."
✓ actually

It flips the sign of the entire answer. Walking clockwise gives −∬ curl dA, not +. And for a region with a hole, "positively oriented" means the outer boundary counterclockwise and the inner boundary clockwise — so that the region is always on your left as you walk. Getting that inner orientation backwards is the most common error in annulus problems.

✕ the myth
"The two forms of Green's theorem are different theorems."
✓ actually

They are the same statement applied to a rotated field. Replace F = ⟨P, Q⟩ with the field turned ninety degrees, ⟨−Q, P⟩, and the circulation form becomes the flux form exactly. That is why curl and div keep appearing as a pair, and why one picture — the tangential and the normal component of the same field — carries both.

✕ the myth
"If the circulation around a loop is zero, the field is conservative."
✓ actually

Zero circulation around one particular loop proves nothing. Conservative requires zero circulation around every closed loop in the region. The field ⟨x², xy⟩ has circulation ∬ y dA, which vanishes over any region symmetric about the x-axis and is non-zero over others. One lucky loop is not a proof.
