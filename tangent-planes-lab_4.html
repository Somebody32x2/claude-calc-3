Tangent planes & linearization

Here is the idea the whole of differential calculus is built on: up close, every smooth surface looks flat. A curved graph, seen through a strong enough magnifier, is indistinguishable from a plane — and that plane is completely determined by the two partial slopes you already know how to find. Replace the surface by its plane and hard questions about f turn into arithmetic.

z = f(a,b) + fx(x−a) + fy(y−b)
One plane, two slopes

Start at the height f(a,b). Tilt east by the x-slope, tilt north by the y-slope. That single tilted plane is the tangent plane — nothing else is needed to pin it down.

f ≈ L near (a, b)
Trade curve for line

Call the plane's height L(x,y), the linearization. Near the point it stands in for f so well that you can compute with it by hand and barely notice the difference.

error ∝ (distance)²
Why it works

Step away and the gap between surface and plane grows only as the square of your distance. Halve the distance and the error drops fourfold — so close in, it is negligible.

01

The plane that just touches

Move the point and watch the mint plane re-tilt to rest flush against the surface. Its two tilts are exactly ∂f/∂x and ∂f/∂y — the coral and teal tangent lines you met in Lab 01 lie inside it.

Where does the formula come from? A plane through the point (a, b, f(a,b)) is z = f(a,b) + M(x−a) + N(y−b) for some tilts M and N. For the plane to touch the surface — to share its slope, not just its height — walking east along the plane must climb at the same rate as walking east along the surface. Walking east on the plane gives slope M; walking east on the surface gives ∂f/∂x. So M = fx, and likewise N = fy. The tangent plane is just the two Calc-1 tangent lines, stitched into one flat sheet.

02

Zoom in until it's flat

This is the payoff. Shrink the window around the point and the surface visibly settles onto the plane. The claim "differentiable = locally linear" is not a slogan here — it is a thing you can watch happen.

Keep your eye on the mint plane: as you zoom, it never changes — its tilt is fixed by the two slopes at the centre. What changes is the surface, which peels down toward the plane. The colour shows the signed gap f − L: coral where the surface rides above the plane, blue where it dips below. Watch the readout — each time you zoom in by 4×, the largest gap shrinks by roughly 16×. That factor-of-square is the whole reason linearization is worth anything.

03

Using it to estimate

Now the point of it all. To guess f at a nearby spot, don't touch f — evaluate the plane instead. That is linear approximation, and the shortcut has a name: the differential.

Rearrange the linearization into the change it predicts. Moving by (dx, dy) from the point, the plane rises by dz = fx dx + fy dy. That is the differential — the linear estimate of the true change Δf = f(a+dx, b+dy) − f(a,b). It is the same tool an engineer uses to turn "my radius is off by a millimetre" into "so my volume is off by this much," without recomputing anything. Nudge the offset and compare the estimate against the truth.

04

Computing them by hand

The recipe never changes. Pick a nearby point (a, b) where f is easy. Find f(a,b), fx, fy. Then L = f(a,b) + fx(x−a) + fy(y−b), and plug in your actual point.

Estimate √(3.98² + 3.03²)
f = √(x² + y²), base (4, 3)
basef(4,3) = √25 = 5, a round anchor
∇f(x/f, y/f) = (4/5, 3/5)
plugdx = −0.02, dy = +0.03
→ 5 + 0.8(−0.02) + 0.6(0.03)
→ 5.002 (true: 5.00213…)
Estimate f(2.05, 2.97)
f = x²y, base (2, 3)
basef(2,3) = 4·3 = 12
∇f(2xy, x²) = (12, 4)
plugdx = 0.05, dy = −0.03
→ 12 + 12(0.05) + 4(−0.03)
→ 12.48 (true: 12.481…)
Error propagation
V = πr²h, r = 5±0.1, h = 10
differentialdV = 2πrh·dr + πr²·dh
only r wobblesdh = 0, dr = 0.1
→ dV = 2π(5)(10)(0.1)
→ = 10π ≈ 31.4
a 2% radius error → a 4% volume error, since dV/V = 2 dr/r

The mistake everyone makes once. Trusting the linearization too far from the base point. The estimate is only as good as the surface is flat, and "flat enough" depends on how sharply f curves — a gentle plane stays accurate for a long way, a tight ripple goes wrong almost immediately. When an estimate has to be trusted, the honest question is never "is L close?" but "how big is the second derivative I'm ignoring?" That curvature term is exactly what the next lab turns into a tool.
