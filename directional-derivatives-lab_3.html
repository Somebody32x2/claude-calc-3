Directional derivatives

Lab 01 let you walk two directions: due east, due north. But you are standing on a hillside and can face any way you like. Pick a heading, walk that way, and ask the same old question — how steep is it? The surprise is that you never need to re-measure. Those two original slopes already contain the answer for every direction at once.

u = (cos θ, sin θ)
Choose a heading

A direction is a unit vector — length exactly one. That length is not a formality: it is what makes the answer a slope rather than a slope times an arbitrary scale.

Du f = ∇f · u
Dot it with the gradient

One dot product and you are done. No new limit, no new slice to differentiate — the two partials you already computed do all the work.

Du f = |∇f| cos φ
It's just a cosine

Where φ is the angle between your heading and the gradient. Steepest when φ = 0, flat when φ = 90°, steepest descent at 180°. One formula explains all three.

02

Every direction at once

Instead of plotting the slope against θ, lay each answer along the direction it belongs to. The shape that appears is not a coincidence.

Each violet spoke is one heading

Its length is the slope you get walking that way. Spokes past the halfway line are downhill — they carry a negative slope, drawn pointing backwards.

The tips trace a perfect circle

And the gradient is its diameter. That is the geometric content of Du f = ∇f · u: projecting one fixed vector onto every possible direction sweeps out a circle through the origin.

The circle touches the origin twice

Those two headings are exactly perpendicular to the gradient, and they are the directions where the ground is level. Everything to one side climbs; everything to the other descends.

03

Why level curves run perpendicular

The map view makes the last claim visible. The level curve through your point is drawn in white — set your heading level and watch your arrow lie down flat along it.

The white curve is your contour

Every point on it has exactly the height you are standing at. Walking along it, your altitude never changes — so the slope in that direction must be zero.

The gradient cuts it at a right angle

If Du f = ∇f · u is zero along the curve, then ∇f is perpendicular to the curve's tangent — that is what a zero dot product means. The gradient never runs along a contour; it always bolts straight across.

Your heading, and its shadow

The faint violet line is your slope projected onto the gradient. When it collapses to nothing you are on the contour; when it reaches full length you are pointing straight uphill.

04

Computing them by hand

Three steps, always the same. Find ∇f at the point. Turn your direction into a unit vector. Take the dot product. The only step anyone ever skips is the middle one.

The mistake everyone makes once. Forgetting to normalize. If you dot the gradient with a raw direction vector like (3, 4) instead of (3/5, 4/5), you get an answer five times too large — and nothing in the arithmetic warns you. A directional derivative is a rate per unit of distance travelled; feeding in a vector of length 5 quietly measures five units of travel per step. Check |u| = 1 before you dot, every time.

05

Common misconceptions

The gradient is the most frequently misunderstood object in the course, and almost all of the trouble comes from one question: which space does it live in?

✕ the myth
"The gradient is an arrow lying on the surface, pointing up the hill in 3-D."
✓ actually

∇f is a two-dimensional vector living in the xy-plane — down on the map, not up on the landscape. It has components (fx, fy) and no z-component at all. Read it as a compass instruction: "from where you're standing, face this way to climb fastest." The steepness of that climb is its length, |∇f|, which is a separate number — not the arrow's third coordinate. This is why every gradient arrow in this lab is drawn flat on the floor of the box and on the map, never tilted up the hillside.

✕ the myth
"The gradient is perpendicular to the surface."
✓ actually

It is perpendicular to the level curves, in the domain. That is a statement about the flat contour map, not about the 3-D surface. (There is a true "normal to the surface" vector, and for the graph z = f(x,y) it works out to (fx, fy, −1) — related to the gradient, but a different object in a different space. Conflating the two is the single most common gradient error.)

✕ the myth
"On a perfectly symmetric bowl, every direction is equally steep."
✓ actually

Rotational symmetry relates different points, not different directions at one point. Standing on the wall of a funnel, walking straight down is steep while walking around the funnel keeps you at exactly the same height — that level direction is the 90° one. The only place all directions truly tie is the very bottom, where ∇f = 0 and the cosine law gives 0 · cos φ = 0 for every heading.

✕ the myth
"Du f is a vector, since it came from vectors."
✓ actually

It is a single number — a slope. A dot product of two vectors always returns a scalar. The direction information was consumed by the dot product: you supplied a heading and got back how fast the height changes along it. If your answer to a directional-derivative question has components, something has gone wrong.

✕ the myth
"If Du f = 0, the point must be a critical point."
✓ actually

It only means that particular heading is level — you happened to be facing along a contour. On any non-flat hillside there are always two such directions, and they say nothing about the point being special. A critical point needs Du f = 0 in every direction at once, which is the same as ∇f = 0.

✕ the myth
"Any direction vector will do — the formula is just a dot product."
✓ actually

The vector must be a unit vector, or the answer is scaled by its length and is no longer a rate per unit distance. Dotting with (3,4) instead of (3/5, 4/5) gives an answer five times too big, and nothing in the arithmetic warns you. Check |u| = 1 before you dot, every single time.
