Calc 3 · Field Lab 01

Partial derivatives

Picture the surface z = f(x, y) as a landscape. A partial derivative just asks: how steep is the ground if I walk in one direction? Walk due east and you get ∂f/∂x. Walk due north and you get ∂f/∂y. That's the whole idea — everything below is a way to feel it.

∂f / ∂x
Slope walking east

Freeze y. Slice the surface and read the slope of the curve you exposed. It's an ordinary Calc 1 derivative — in the x-direction only.

∂f / ∂y
Slope walking north

Freeze x instead. Same move, perpendicular direction. One surface, two independent slopes, one at each point.

∇f = (∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y)
The steepest way up

Stack the two slopes into a vector. It points straight uphill, and its length is how steep that steepest climb is.

02

Why it's just Calc 1

Each partial is the slope of one of these flat cross-sections. Freeze a variable and the surface collapses into a single curve you already know how to differentiate.

Hold y fixed → slope is ∂f/∂x—

The east–west cross-section through your point. Its tangent slope is the x-partial.

Hold x fixed → slope is ∂f/∂y—

The north–south cross-section. Same surface, perpendicular cut, a different slope.

03

Looking straight down

Flatten the landscape into a topographic map. The gold gradient arrow is built from your two partials — and it always points across the bands, straight uphill.

The gold arrow is ∇f

Its components are exactly the numbers on your instrument panel: ∂f/∂x points east, ∂f/∂y points north, and together they aim at the fastest way up.

Each color band is one elevation

Everywhere inside a band sits at nearly the same height. Move along a band and your height barely changes — that direction has almost zero slope.

Tight bands mean steep ground

Where the colors change fast over a short distance, the gradient is long. Wide bands mean gentle terrain and a short arrow.

04

Computing them by hand

Two ways of writing the same thing
∂f/∂xThe Leibniz form. Read it as "the rate of change of f as x varies, with y held still". The curly ∂ is what announces that something else is being held fixed.
fxThe subscript form — identical meaning, just shorter. The subscript names which variable you differentiated with respect to. It is not a coordinate, a component, or an index into a list.
fySame idea, differentiating with respect to y and holding x still.
∇fBoth partials stacked into one vector, ⟨fx, fy⟩, always in that order. Spoken "grad f" or "del f".
Don't read the subscript as multiplication or a label. fx is not "f times x" and not "the x-part of f" — it is an instruction that has already been carried out: a whole new function, produced by differentiating. Later labs stack subscripts (fxx, fxy) to mean "differentiate again", and the order they are listed in will matter.

Why two notations exist at all. Leibniz form is unambiguous and self-documenting, so it wins in derivations and when several variables are in play. Subscript form is compact, so it wins in formulas that would otherwise be unreadable — the tangent plane, the discriminant, the Hessian. You need to be fluent switching between them, because textbooks and lecturers swap freely, often mid-sentence.

The mechanical rule is short: to get ∂f/∂x (equivalently fx), treat every other variable as a constant and differentiate normally. Then do the same for ∂f/∂y. No new differentiation rules — just a new habit about what's holding still.

f = x²y + 3y
∂/∂xy is a constant, so 3y vanishes
→ 2xy
∂/∂yx² is a constant coefficient here
→ x² + 3
f = sin(xy)
∂/∂xchain rule; inner deriv w.r.t x is y
→ y·cos(xy)
∂/∂ynow the inner deriv is x
→ x·cos(xy)
f = x / y
∂/∂x1/y is just a constant multiplier
→ 1 / y
∂/∂yrewrite as x·y⁻¹, power rule
→ −x / y²

The whole procedure, slowly: find ∇f for f = x²y + 3y and evaluate it at (2, −1)

Nothing skipped. This is exactly what belongs on paper, including the substitution of actual numbers at the end.

  1. Differentiate with respect to x, holding y absolutely still. Go term by term. In x²y, the y is a constant coefficient sitting in front of x² — so differentiate x² and keep the y. The term 3y contains no x at all, so it is a pure constant and dies.
    ∂/∂x [ x²·y ] = (2x)·y = 2xy
    ∂/∂x [ 3y ] = 0   (no x present)
    fx = 2xy
  2. Now differentiate with respect to y, holding x still. This time x² is the constant coefficient and y is the variable. Both terms are linear in y, so both survive.
    ∂/∂y [ x²·y ] = x²·(1) = x²
    ∂/∂y [ 3y ] = 3
    fy = x² + 3
  3. Assemble the gradient. It is just the two partials stacked into a vector — in that order, always x first.
    ∇f = ⟨ fx, fy ⟩ = ⟨ 2xy, x² + 3 ⟩
    Note this is still a function: a different vector at every point.
  4. Evaluate at the point (2, −1). Substitute x = 2 and y = −1 into each component separately. Watch the signs.
    fx(2, −1) = 2·(2)·(−1) = −4
    fy(2, −1) = (2)² + 3 = 4 + 3 = 7
    ∇f(2, −1) = ⟨−4, 7⟩
  5. Read what it means. The x-slope is −4: walking east from (2, −1), the surface falls steeply. The y-slope is +7: walking north, it climbs even more steeply. The gradient ⟨−4, 7⟩ points north-west-ish — the fastest way uphill — and the steepest rate is its length:
    |∇f| = √( (−4)² + 7² ) = √(16 + 49) = √65 ≈ 8.06
  6. One chain-rule case, since it trips people up. For g = sin(xy), the outer function is sine and the inner is xy. Differentiate outer, then multiply by the derivative of the inner with respect to the variable you're using:
    ∂/∂x: inner derivative is ∂(xy)/∂x = y
       ⟹ gx = cos(xy) · y = y cos(xy)
    ∂/∂y: inner derivative is ∂(xy)/∂y = x
       ⟹ gy = cos(xy) · x = x cos(xy)
    The only difference between the two lines is which letter the inner derivative produced.
05

Common misconceptions

Each of these sounds perfectly reasonable, and each one quietly breaks something later. They are worth reading before you need them.

✕ the myth
"∂f/∂x is the slope of the surface at that point."
✓ actually

A surface has infinitely many slopes at a single point — one for every direction you could face. ∂f/∂x is just the east–west one, and ∂f/∂y the north–south one. They are two samples out of a whole circle of possible answers. Getting the slope in an arbitrary direction is the job of the directional derivative.

✕ the myth
"∂ is just a fancier-looking d."
✓ actually

The curly ∂ carries real information: it announces that everything else is being held fixed. Plain d is reserved for when there is only one variable, or when the other variables are themselves changing along with it. The moment y secretly depends on x, the rules change and you need the chain rule — which is precisely why the notation distinguishes the two cases.

✕ the myth
"If both partials are zero, the point is a maximum or a minimum."
✓ actually

Flat is not the same as extreme. On the saddle above, both partials vanish at the origin, yet the ground climbs to the east and falls to the north — it is neither a peak nor a pit. Zero gradient makes a point a suspect, not a verdict. Deciding which kind it is takes second derivatives.

✕ the myth
"The two partials tell you everything about the surface near that point."
✓ actually

They tell you everything about the surface's flat approximation near that point — the tangent plane — and nothing at all about how it curves away from it. A bowl and a saddle can have identical partials at a point and behave completely differently a step away. Curvature is invisible to first derivatives by construction.
