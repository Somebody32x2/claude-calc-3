Polar coordinates & the Jacobian

Some regions are circles, and forcing a square grid onto a circle is misery. Polar coordinates lay a grid of rings and rays instead — a perfect fit. But there's a price, and it is the most important detail in the whole subject: a polar tile is not dr dθ in area. It's r dr dθ, and that stray r is a preview of the Jacobian that governs every change of variables.

x = r cos θ,  y = r sin θ
Rings and rays

Locate a point by how far out it is (r) and which way it faces (θ). Circles become r = const and the messy limits melt away.

dA = r dr dθ
Tiles grow with r

A tile spanning the same dr and dθ is tiny near the centre and broad far out — because its outer arc is longer. Its area carries a factor of r.

∬ f dA = ∬ f·|J| du dv
The Jacobian, in general

Any coordinate change stretches area by a local factor |J| — the determinant of the map's derivative. For polar, |J| = r. Same idea, every time.

01

The grid of rings and rays

Here is the polar grid laid over the plane. Slide the highlighted tile outward and watch it grow — same step in r, same step in θ, but a bigger and bigger patch of actual area.

Every tile below spans one step of r and one step of θ. Near the centre they are slivers; near the rim they are broad panels. That is the entire phenomenon: equal steps in the coordinates do not mean equal areas. A square grid never has this problem — every cell is dx dy — which is exactly why the correction factor is invisible until you leave Cartesian coordinates.

02

Where the extra r comes from

Blow one tile up. It's a slice of a ring — two straight radial edges and two circular arcs. Its area is pure geometry, and in the limit it lands exactly on r dr dθ.

The tile runs from radius r to r + dr, through an angle dθ. A full ring of angular width dθ is the fraction dθ / 2π of an annulus, so its area is that fraction of π(r+dr)² − πr²:

area = ½[(r+dr)² − r²] dθ = ½[2r·dr + dr²] dθ = (r + ½dr) dr dθ

The inner edge has arc length r dθ, the outer edge (r+dr)dθ — the average width is (r + ½dr)dθ, and times the height dr that's the area of the little near-rectangle. As dr → 0 the ½dr vanishes and you are left with the clean r dr dθ. Shrink the sliders and watch the exact area and r dr dθ converge.

03

The Jacobian is an area-stretch factor

Step back and see the general machine. On the left, a plain rectangular grid in (r, θ). On the right, where each cell lands in (x, y). The map warps a uniform grid into fans — and the local warp factor is exactly r.

A change of variables is a map from one plane to another. Take a tiny cell of area dr dθ in the (r, θ) plane; it lands as some little quadrilateral in the (x, y) plane, with a different area. The ratio of those areas is the Jacobian determinant — the determinant of the matrix of partial derivatives of the map:

J = ∂(x,y)/∂(r,θ) = | xr  xθ ; yr  yθ | = | cos θ  −r sin θ ; sin θ  r cos θ | = r(cos²θ + sin²θ) = r

Pick a cell and read the two areas straight off the panels. Their ratio is the mid-radius of that cell — small near the centre, large near the rim — which is the Jacobian |J| = r measured by hand. That is the entire reason the r appears in dA = r dr dθ: it is the factor by which this particular map stretches area.

04

Integrating over a disk

Now cash it in. Add up f over a disk with a polar grid — every tile weighted by its true area r dr dθ. As the grid refines, the sum locks onto the exact value the r makes possible.

To integrate over the disk of radius 2, sweep θ from 0 to 2π and r from 0 to 2, weighting each tile by r dr dθ. Refine the grid and the Riemann sum converges on the exact integral. Drop the r and it converges on the wrong number — the factor isn't optional bookkeeping, it's the area.

The great payoff is the Gaussian. The integral of e^−(x²+y²) over the whole plane has no elementary antiderivative in x and y — but in polar it is ∫₀^{2π}∫₀^∞ e^−r² · r dr dθ, and that lone r is exactly what a u = r² substitution needs. It collapses to 2π · ½ = π. Choose e^−r² above and grow the radius in your mind: the disk value climbs toward π.

The mistake everyone makes once. Converting the integrand to polar but forgetting to convert the area element — writing ∫∫ f(r,θ) dr dθ instead of ∫∫ f(r,θ) r dr dθ. It is the single most common error in all of multivariable calculus, and the "Without the r" readout above shows exactly how wrong it goes: it systematically under-weights the outer rings, which is where most of a disk's area actually lives. When you change variables, the r — or in general the |J| — is not decoration. It is the new area.
